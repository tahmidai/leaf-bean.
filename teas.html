World of Tea

Explore the diverse varieties, flavors, and traditions of tea from around the globe.

Types of Tea

All true tea comes from the Camellia sinensis plant. The differences in flavor, color, and aroma arise from variations in processing methods, oxidation levels, and growing conditions.

Green Tea

Minimally oxidized, green tea retains its natural green color and fresh, grassy flavor. Popular varieties include Japanese Sencha, Matcha, and Chinese Dragon Well. Rich in antioxidants, particularly catechins.

Flavor profile: Grassy, vegetal, slightly sweet or nutty

Brewing temp: 160-180°F (70-82°C)

Steeping time: 2-3 minutes

Black Tea

Fully oxidized, black tea offers robust flavor and dark color. Popular varieties include Assam, Darjeeling, Earl Grey, and English Breakfast. Contains more caffeine than other teas.

Flavor profile: Malty, robust, sometimes smoky or fruity

Brewing temp: 200-212°F (93-100°C)

Steeping time: 3-5 minutes

Oolong Tea

Partially oxidized, oolong represents a spectrum between green and black tea. Taiwanese High Mountain Oolong and Chinese Tieguanyin are prized varieties.

Flavor profile: Floral, fruity, creamy, with honey notes

Brewing temp: 185-205°F (85-96°C)

Steeping time: 3-5 minutes

Herbal Tea & Chai

Not technically "tea" as they don't come from Camellia sinensis, but infusions of herbs, spices, flowers, and fruits. Chai is a spiced milk tea blend from India.

Flavor profile: Varies widely by ingredients

Brewing temp: 200-212°F (93-100°C)

Steeping time: 5-7 minutes

Tea Culture

Chinese Tea Traditions

China has thousands of years of tea history. The Gongfu tea ceremony emphasizes skill and artistry, using small clay teapots for multiple infusions to fully appreciate the tea's evolving flavors.

Different regions produce distinct varieties: Fujian for oolongs like Tieguanyin, Yunnan for pu-erh (fermented tea), and Zhejiang for Dragon Well green tea.
